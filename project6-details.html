Towards Achieving Land Degradation Neutrality

Comprehensive Restoration Report

Duration: 2018 - 2021 Funding: UNEP & Green Belt Movement - $900K Role: Environmental Restoration Lead

Executive Summary

This transformative project demonstrates how integrated landscape restoration can achieve Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN) while enhancing food security and building climate resilience. Through community-driven approaches in Tharaka-Nithi County, we successfully restored 5,000 hectares of degraded land, benefiting 15,000 households and creating a replicable model for sustainable land management across Kenya's semi-arid regions.

Key Project Outcomes

  • Restored 5,000 hectares of degraded agricultural and pastoral land
  • Planted 2.5 million indigenous and drought-resistant trees
  • Established 300+ community-based restoration groups
  • Achieved 40% increase in agricultural productivity and 50,000 tons annual CO2 sequestration

Project Background & Context

Study Area

Tharaka-Nithi County is a semi-arid region in central Kenya covering 2,609 kmÂ² with a population of 390,000. The area experiences erratic rainfall (400-1,200mm annually) and has suffered extensive land degradation affecting 60% of agricultural land, threatening food security and livelihoods.

Degradation Challenges

  • Severe soil erosion affecting 70% of farmland
  • Deforestation reducing forest cover to 15%
  • Declining crop yields and livestock productivity
  • Limited water retention and storage capacity
  • Poverty affecting 55% of households

Project Objectives

  • Achieve Land Degradation Neutrality at landscape scale
  • Restore ecosystem services and biodiversity
  • Enhance food security and rural livelihoods
  • Build community capacity for sustainable land management
  • Create replicable restoration models

Methodology & Implementation Approach

Phase 1: Community Mobilization & Planning

Comprehensive community engagement and participatory planning to ensure local ownership and sustainable restoration practices.

Community Mapping

  • Participatory mapping of degraded areas
  • Traditional knowledge documentation
  • Stakeholder analysis and engagement strategy
  • Baseline socio-economic assessments

Capacity Building

  • Training 5,000 farmers in sustainable practices
  • Establishing community-based organizations
  • Technical skills development programs
  • Leadership and governance training

Phase 2: Integrated Restoration Implementation

Multi-faceted restoration approach combining agroforestry, soil conservation, water harvesting, and sustainable agriculture practices.

Agroforestry Systems

Integration of indigenous trees with crops and livestock for improved soil health, biodiversity, and income diversification.

Soil & Water Conservation

Construction of terraces, check dams, and water harvesting structures to reduce erosion and improve water retention.

Climate-Smart Agriculture

Introduction of drought-resistant crops, conservation agriculture, and sustainable farming practices.

Phase 3: Monitoring & Sustainability

Comprehensive monitoring and evaluation system using satellite data, ground surveys, and community-based monitoring.

Satellite Monitoring

NDVI and land cover change analysis to track restoration progress

Community Monitoring

Training local monitors to track ecosystem recovery and livelihood improvements

Sustainability Planning

Development of long-term management plans and financing mechanisms

Key Results & Impact

Restoration Results

5,000 Hectares of degraded land restored
2.5M Trees planted (80% survival rate)
75% Reduction in soil erosion rates
60% Increase in vegetation cover

Socio-Economic Impact

Food Security

40% increase in crop yields and 60% improvement in household food security among participating farmers.

Income Generation

Average household income increased by 35% through diversified agriculture, beekeeping, and tree nurseries.

Women Empowerment

Women's participation in land management decisions increased from 30% to 70%, with 150 women's groups established.

Environmental Benefits

Carbon Sequestration

Estimated 50,000 tons of CO2 sequestered annually through forest restoration and improved soil carbon storage.

Biodiversity Recovery

80% increase in native species diversity with return of 25 indigenous tree species and wildlife corridors.

Water Resources

45% reduction in surface runoff and 85% increase in groundwater recharge in restored watersheds.

Lessons Learned & Recommendations

Successful Approaches

Community Ownership

Strong community ownership and participation was the key to successful and sustainable restoration outcomes.

Integrated Landscape Approach

Combining multiple restoration techniques (agroforestry, water conservation, sustainable agriculture) proved more effective than single interventions.

Traditional Knowledge Integration

Blending indigenous practices with modern techniques resulted in culturally appropriate and technically sound solutions.

Key Challenges

Climate Variability

Erratic rainfall patterns required adaptive management and drought-resistant restoration approaches.

Market Access

Limited market access for restoration products required development of new value chains and cooperatives.

Long-term Financing

Securing sustainable financing for maintenance and expansion of restoration activities beyond project period.

Policy Recommendations

  1. National LDN Framework: Develop comprehensive national framework for achieving Land Degradation Neutrality
  2. Payment for Ecosystem Services: Establish PES schemes to incentivize restoration and maintenance
  3. Community Land Rights: Strengthen community land tenure to encourage long-term stewardship
  4. Restoration Finance: Create innovative financing mechanisms for landscape restoration projects
  5. Capacity Building: Scale up technical and institutional capacity for sustainable land management
